Designing for Decision Confidence

— Increasing job applications by removing candidate hesitation, not adding discovery.

— The case study in 30 seconds
TL;DR

01Business problem

  • The company needed to scale from 500 to 5,000 hires a year. The blocker wasn't demand — it was a slow hiring funnel, and the slowest stage was resume sourcing.

02Insight

  • Candidates were finding startups but not applying to them. The failure was at decision confidence, not discoverability — a different problem than a typical job board solves.

03The move

  • I reframed the surface from a "find a job" tool into a "decide on a company" tool — consolidating fragmented company info, surfacing trust signals at the point of doubt, and stripping the apply flow to essentials.

04Outcome

  • Application conversion rose, sourcing time dropped from 2–3 months to 3–5 weeks, and complete profiles converted at ~1.8× the rate of incomplete ones — validating the thesis: trust drives the apply decision.
— The business problem
01

A target that broke the funnel.

The platform is a hiring marketplace for startups. Leadership had a hard target: grow from 500 to 5,000 hires annually over 2–3 years, without proportionally growing cost.

At 500 hires a year, roles were taking 4–6 months to close. At that velocity, 5,000 was arithmetically impossible — the funnel would collapse under its own latency long before headcount scaled.

So the real brief wasn't "redesign the job pages." It was: where in the hiring funnel is time leaking, and can design close the leak without adding headcount?

— Finding the bottleneck
02

Where time leaked.

I analysed 200+ job closures across 50+ startups to locate the delay, stage by stage. The goal state was under 2 months end-to-end.

Sourcing was the single largest block of time and it poisoned everything downstream — a candidate sourced reluctantly is one who later ghosts the offer or quits in 90 days. Hiring data backed this: ~50% offer drop-out, ~40% attrition inside 90 days.

Decision: I scoped the project to resume sourcing. Fixing the first domino had the highest leverage on the business goal and the cleanest causal line to the downstream symptoms.

— Why this hadn't been solved
03

Solving the wrong problem.

Job marketplaces are overwhelmingly optimised for discovery — search, filters, more listings, better matching. That makes sense when the problem is "candidates can't find roles."

But our funnel data said the opposite. Candidates were reaching startup roles. Only 2 in 10 shortlisted candidates were willing to apply. They could see the door; they wouldn't walk through it.

A better search bar can't fix that. The category had been solving for findability when the real failure was confidence. That gap was the opening.

— Strategic frame
04

Metrics that say no for you.

Before designing anything, I fixed the success metrics and guardrails so the team could say no to the wrong ideas.

Explicitly out of scope: the employer-side dashboard, screening automation, scheduling, and anything post-offer. All real problems — but downstream of the bottleneck I was solving. Scoping them out was a decision, not an omission.

North Star
Application conversion at sourcing
Shortlisted → applied. I deliberately did not pick "total applications" — raw volume can be inflated with low-intent clicks that just move the bottleneck downstream.
Guardrail
Candidate fit must not degrade
If conversion rose but interview-to-offer quality dropped, we'd have manufactured noise, not velocity. We watched fit as a counter-metric.
Supporting
The signals beneath
  • Sourcing time
  • Profile views
  • Apply-flow completion
— Research
05

Two passes, two questions.

Qualitative and exploratory: 12+ depth interviews with startup hiring teams to understand the supply side, then 20+ in-depth candidate interviews across the full journey, validated against funnel data with the analytics team.

I ran two passes deliberately: hiring teams told me where time leaked; candidates told me why they hesitated.

~70%
Low trust in startups
Perceived job insecurity, unknown leadership, unstable environment
68%

The problem at resume sourcing was never discoverability. It was hesitation.

— The reframe

From find to decide.

Candidates were doing unpaid, high-effort detective work — stitching a picture of each company together from LinkedIn, news, Glassdoor, and the company site — and abandoning the application when the picture stayed incomplete or untrustworthy.

So I restated the problem the team was solving:

From: "Help candidates find startup roles."
To: "Give candidates enough trust and clarity to decide, in one place, before the doubt sends them away."

Every design decision below is downstream of that single sentence.

— Design principles
06
01

Consolidate, don't scatter

One structured, scannable view per company instead of forcing multi-source research.
02

Answer doubt where it appears

Surface trust signals — funding, team, reviews — exactly where hesitation spikes.
03

Clarity before commitment

Make role and process expectations legible before the apply button.
04

Friction only where it earns intent

Strip the apply flow to essentials; add effort back only where it helps a candidate stand out.
— The solution, mapped to the journey
07
Discover

The structured company profile

Candidates relied on multiple sources to understand a company — high-effort and low-confidence.

Response
  • A single profile consolidating basics, trust signals (reviews, funding), and decision support (active jobs, team & culture) into one scannable view
  • Trade-off: I chose consolidation over a richer "explore" experience — the effort of assembling information, not its absence, was what killed confidence
[ Company profile screen ]
Evaluate

The jobs listing

Candidates struggled to confirm a role was relevant before investing attention.

Response
  • A focused listing surfacing all open roles upfront with search, filters, and scannable cards (location, type, experience, openings, recency)
  • Why: lets a candidate confirm "is there a fit for me here?" before they emotionally invest — protecting against sunk-cost hesitation
[ Jobs listing screen ]
Decide

The job detail page

Candidates lacked clarity on the role, expectations, and hiring process — so they stalled.

Response
  • JD and skills for role clarity; role expectations and the named hiring manager for human trust; a visible step-by-step hiring process for decision confidence
  • Why visible process: uncertainty about "what happens after I apply" was a recurring hesitation trigger that competing offers exploit
[ Job detail screen ]
Act

The apply flow

Complex final steps caused drop-offs at the last moment.

Response
  • A guided flow asking only for essentials (resume), plus an optional intro video as a differentiation nudge ("be in the top 20%")
  • Trade-off: the video is optional, not mandatory — high-intent candidates self-select to stand out without taxing everyone else
[ Apply flow screen ]
— Feature prioritisation: what I cut
08

The discipline of no.

Not everything that tested well shipped. Each cut traces back to the North Star or a guardrail — that's the test I used to say no.

Cut
Salary on every card
  • High candidate demand, but employer-side data was inconsistent and partly out of scope. Surfacing unreliable data would erode the trust we were building.
Deferred
In-profile messaging
  • Appealing, but it shifted the surface from "decide" to "communicate," and risked spam on the employer side.
Kept light
Culture media galleries
  • Research said candidates wanted substance (funding, team, role) over polish; heavy media risked looking like marketing and lowering trust.
— Impact
09

What moved.

I framed measurement as a cohort comparison — the new structured experience against the legacy one. One metric mattered more than the rest, because it isolated the exact variable I set out to design for.

The metric I trust most
1.8×

Complete-information profiles converted candidates at ~1.8× the rate of incomplete ones.

This is the cleanest proof of the thesis. It isolates the single variable I designed for — completeness and trust — and shows it, not discovery, driving the apply decision.

— Learnings
10
01

Trust drives action

Candidates apply when company information is transparent, complete, and legible — not when there's simply more of it.
02

Solve the bottleneck, not the symptom

Offer drop-out and early attrition were downstream of a sourcing problem; fixing the source fixed the symptoms.
03

Reframing is the highest-leverage act

The shift from discoverability to hesitation changed every screen that followed.
04

Unearned friction is the enemy

Remove it at the apply step; reintroduce it only where it helps the candidate win.
What I'd validate next

Does the trust effect compound or saturate?

  • Whether candidates need all signals, or funding + team carry most of the lift
  • Whether the optional intro video improves employer-side shortlisting, or just candidate confidence
  • Whether "confidence over discovery" holds for non-startup employers, where trust is less of a deficit
